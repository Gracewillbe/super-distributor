# Positioning: Own a Spot in Your Users' Minds

> "Positioning is not what you do to a product. It's what you do to the mind of the prospect." — Al Ries & Jack Trout

## The Positioning Problem

You built something. It solves a real problem. But when someone asks "What is it?", you struggle to answer in one sentence.

Or worse, you say something like:
- "It's a platform for..."
- "We help people..."
- "It's like X, but better..."

**Here's the truth: If you can't define what you are in the user's mind, they won't remember you.**

The market is noisy. Users are overwhelmed. Their mental capacity is limited. They won't spend time figuring out what makes you different.

**If you don't position yourself, the market will position you — or forget you entirely.**

---

## What Positioning Actually Is

Positioning is not your tagline. It's not your feature list. It's not what you think you are.

**Positioning is the unique spot you own in the user's mind.**

When someone has a problem, do they think of you? Do they know what makes you different from alternatives? Can they explain you to someone else in one sentence?

That's positioning.

### Classic Examples

- **Volvo** = Safety (not "reliable Swedish cars")
- **7-Up** = The Un-Cola (not "lemon-lime soda")
- **Zoom** = Video calls that just work (not "enterprise communication platform")

Each owns a **single, clear concept** in the user's mind. Simple. Memorable. Different.

---

## Why Positioning Comes Before Everything Else

Here's the sequence most indie builders get wrong:

❌ **Wrong order:**
1. Build all the features
2. Write marketing copy
3. Launch everywhere
4. Wonder why no one cares

✅ **Right order:**
1. **Position first** — Define what you are
2. Build to support that position
3. Market to reinforce that position
4. Attract users who need exactly that

**Positioning is the foundation.** Get it wrong, and everything else is built on sand.

## The Three-Step Positioning Framework

### Step 1: Find the Problem Users Already Know

Positioning starts with a problem **users already recognize**.

Not a problem you invented. Not a problem you have to educate them about. A problem they're already complaining about, searching for, and trying to solve.

**Ask yourself:**

1. **What problem are users already talking about?**
   - Look at communities (X, Reddit, GitHub, Discord)
   - What do they complain about repeatedly?
   - What solutions do they say "suck"?

2. **Is this problem urgent or nice-to-have?**
   - Urgent = they'll try imperfect solutions
   - Nice-to-have = they'll wait for the perfect one (bad for indie makers)

3. **Are existing solutions failing them?**
   - Too expensive?
   - Too complicated?
   - Built for enterprise, not indie makers?
   - Missing a specific use case?

**Example: Super Distributor**

**Problem users already know:**
- "I built something but have zero users"
- "I don't know how to do marketing"
- "Distribution guides are for big companies, not solo founders"

**Urgent?** Yes. They just spent months building. They need users now.

**Existing solutions failing?** Yes. Most marketing advice is for companies with budgets and teams.

---

### Step 2: Map the Competitive Landscape

You're not the first person to solve this problem. Other solutions exist — some direct competitors, some indirect substitutes.

**Your job: Figure out what positions are already taken in the user's mind.**

#### A. List the Alternatives

What are users currently doing to solve this problem?

- **Direct competitors** (other products in your category)
- **Indirect substitutes** (different approaches to the same problem)
- **Do-it-yourself** (users solving it manually)

#### B. Identify What They Own

For each alternative, ask: **What's their positioning?**

Not what they say about themselves. What users think of them.

**Example: Distribution for Indie Makers**

| Alternative | Position in User's Mind |
|-------------|-------------------------|
| "Growth hacking" articles | Theory, not actionable |
| Marketing agencies | Expensive, for companies with budgets |
| Influencer advice | "Just build an audience first" (not helpful) |
| DIY trial-and-error | Slow, lonely, hit-or-miss |

#### C. Find the Gap

Look for:
- **Positions no one owns** (white space in the market)
- **Positions owned weakly** (competitors claim it but don't deliver)
- **Positions abandoned** (used to exist, now neglected)

**Example: Super Distributor's Gap**

- Most resources are **theory** (not step-by-step playbooks)
- Most resources assume **budget and team** (not solo founders)
- No one is **open-sourcing distribution knowledge** the way devs open-source code

**Gap found:** Practical, executable, open-source distribution for indie makers.

---

### Step 3: Stake Your Claim

Now you know:
1. The problem users recognize
2. What positions competitors own
3. Where the gap is

**Time to define your unique position.**

#### A. Craft Your Positioning Statement

Use this format:

```
For [target user],
who [has this specific problem],
[Your product] is [category]
that [unique differentiator].

Unlike [main alternative],
[Your product] [key difference].
```

**Example: Super Distributor**

```
For indie makers and solo founders,
who just built something but have zero users,
Super Distributor is an open-source distribution guide
that provides step-by-step playbooks instead of generic advice.

Unlike marketing blogs and growth hacking articles,
Super Distributor is built by builders, for builders — practical, not theoretical.
```

#### B. Distill to One Sentence

The positioning statement is internal. Your **positioning line** is external — the one-sentence version users will remember.

**Test: Can you say it in one breath?**

Examples:
- ❌ Too vague: "We help people grow their products"
- ❌ Too complex: "An open-source collection of playbooks, prompts, and tools for distribution"
- ✅ Clear: "Distribution playbooks for indie makers"

**Super Distributor's positioning line:**
> "The open-source growth playbook for indie makers who can build but can't distribute."

Simple. Clear. Explains who it's for and what it does.

#### C. Validate Your Position

Before you lock it in, test it:

1. **The 5-second test**
   - Show your positioning line to someone who doesn't know your product
   - Wait 5 seconds
   - Ask them to repeat what you do
   - If they can't, it's too complex

2. **The "why you?" test**
   - Ask a potential user: "Why would you use this instead of [competitor]?"
   - Their answer should match your differentiator
   - If they say "I don't know," your positioning isn't clear

3. **The memory test**
   - A week later, ask someone what your product does
   - If they forgot or confused it with something else, your positioning isn't sticky

## Executing Your Positioning

Positioning isn't a one-time exercise. It's a commitment.

### 1. Align Everything to Your Position

Once you claim a position, **every** touchpoint should reinforce it:

- **Product features** → Support the position (cut features that don't)
- **Marketing copy** → Repeat the position (landing page, tweets, docs)
- **Community engagement** → Demonstrate the position (answer questions, share playbooks)

**Consistency is how you own the position in users' minds.**

### 2. Resist the Temptation to Expand Too Soon

Positioning works because it's **narrow**. The narrower, the sharper.

"Distribution for indie makers" is stronger than "Distribution for everyone."

**Don't dilute your position by trying to be everything to everyone.**

Start narrow. Own that niche. Expand later **only if** you can maintain clarity.

### 3. Know When to Evolve (Not Pivot)

Positioning can evolve as your product matures, but it should be **deliberate, not reactive**.

**Good reasons to evolve:**
- You've saturated your initial niche (e.g., "indie makers" → "small teams")
- User needs have shifted (e.g., "landing pages" → "full growth stack")
- A new gap opens in the market

**Bad reasons to change:**
- Competitor launches something new (don't chase)
- You're bored with your position (users aren't)
- One user asked for something off-brand (don't let outliers derail you)

**Rule:** Evolve your positioning along a logical path. Don't jump to a completely different position.

**Example:**
- ✅ "Distribution for indie makers" → "Growth playbooks for small teams" (natural expansion)
- ❌ "Distribution for indie makers" → "Enterprise marketing automation" (confusing pivot)

## Common Positioning Mistakes

### Mistake 1: "We're better than X"

**Problem:** "Better" is not a position. It's a comparison.

Users don't remember "better." They remember "different."

**Fix:** Define what makes you **different**, not better.

- ❌ "A better project management tool"
- ✅ "Project management for remote teams" (different context)

---

### Mistake 2: "We do everything"

**Problem:** Trying to own multiple positions confuses users.

Users can only hold one concept per brand in their minds.

**Fix:** Pick **one thing** to be known for. Add features, but don't add positioning.

- ❌ "We do email marketing, social media, SEO, and analytics"
- ✅ "Email marketing for e-commerce" (one thing)

---

### Mistake 3: Positioning against yourself

**Problem:** Your messaging contradicts your position.

Example: You position as "simple and fast," but your landing page lists 50 features.

**Fix:** Audit all messaging. Cut anything that doesn't reinforce the position.

---

### Mistake 4: Changing positioning too often

**Problem:** Repositioning every few months because you're not seeing traction.

**Reality:** Positioning takes time to settle in users' minds. Changing it too soon means starting over.

**Fix:** Commit for at least 6 months. If it's truly not working, then evolve — but don't pivot weekly.

---

## Your Positioning Checklist

Before you move to distribution, answer these:

- [ ] I can explain what I do in **one sentence**
- [ ] I know the **specific problem** users already recognize
- [ ] I've identified **what positions competitors own**
- [ ] I've found a **gap or weak spot** I can claim
- [ ] My positioning is **different**, not just "better"
- [ ] A stranger can repeat my positioning after hearing it once
- [ ] All my marketing reinforces **one core position**, not five

**If you can't check all these, you're not positioned. Go back and sharpen it.**

## Real-World Positioning in Action

### Case Study: Super Distributor

**Problem recognized by users:**
"I built something but have zero users. I don't know how to do marketing."

**Competitive landscape:**
- Growth hacking blogs (theory, not action)
- Marketing agencies (expensive, not for indies)
- Influencer advice ("build an audience first" — unhelpful)

**Gap found:**
No one is providing **step-by-step, open-source, builder-to-builder** distribution guidance.

**Position claimed:**
"Open-source distribution playbooks for indie makers who can build but can't distribute."

**Differentiation:**
- Open-source (vs. paid courses)
- Playbooks (vs. theory)
- Builder-to-builder (vs. marketing agency speak)

**Execution:**
- Repository structure = playbooks (not blog posts)
- Free and open (not gated content)
- Built in public (not polished corporate launch)

**Result:**
Clear, memorable, different. Users know exactly what it is and why it's not like everything else.
