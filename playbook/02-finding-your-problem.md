# Finding Your Problem: Spot the Structural Imbalance

> "The best businesses aren't built by solving small problems well. They're built by finding big problems that others can't or won't solve."

## Before You Build, Find the Imbalance

Most indie makers start backwards:

1. "I have an idea for a product"
2. Build it
3. Try to find users
4. Wonder why no one cares

**Here's the right order:**

1. **Find a structural imbalance** (supply and demand are mismatched)
2. Validate it's big enough and you're the right person to solve it
3. Position yourself uniquely
4. Build to support that position
5. Distribute to the people who need it

This playbook is about **step 1** — finding problems worth solving.

---

## What is a Structural Imbalance?

A structural imbalance isn't just "people want something they don't have." That's a surface-level pain point.

**A structural imbalance is:**

A place where **demand and supply are fundamentally mismatched**, and that mismatch is **systematic, not temporary**.

### The Three Components

**1. Strong demand exists**
- People need this (not just "want")
- The need is frequent or urgent
- The problem is getting worse or staying constant, not going away

**2. Supply is inadequate**
- Existing solutions don't work well
- Solutions are too expensive / complex / inaccessible
- No one is serving a specific segment

**3. The gap is structural**
- This won't fix itself
- The imbalance persists over time
- New solutions can't easily eliminate it (there's a moat)

**Example: Super Distributor**

**Demand:**
- Thousands of indie makers ship products every month
- Most have zero users after launch
- They desperately need distribution help

**Supply:**
- Marketing advice is for companies with budgets
- Growth hacking blogs are theory, not action
- No one provides step-by-step, open-source playbooks for solo founders

**Gap:**
- As AI makes building easier, more people will ship products
- Distribution becomes the bottleneck (gap gets bigger)
- This is systematic, not temporary

**Imbalance found.** Now validate if it's worth solving.

## The Three Validation Questions

You found an imbalance. Before you commit, ask three questions:

### Question 1: Is the Problem Big Enough?

Not every imbalance is worth solving. Small problems → small businesses. Big problems → big opportunities.

**Three dimensions of "big enough":**

#### A. Market Size
- **How many people have this problem?**
- Use Market Intelligence (Playbook 01) to estimate
- Look for communities, search volumes, forum discussions

**Red flags:**
- ❌ "I'm the only one with this problem" (too small)
- ❌ "This affects a few hundred people max" (niche can work, but know the limits)

**Green lights:**
- ✅ Thousands of people complain about this regularly
- ✅ Multiple communities exist around this pain point
- ✅ Existing (bad) solutions have significant user bases

#### B. Frequency & Urgency
- **How often do people encounter this problem?**
- **How painful is it when they do?**

**The matrix:**

| Frequency | Urgency | Viability |
|-----------|---------|-----------|
| High frequency + High urgency | ✅ Best case (e.g., "no users after launch") |
| High frequency + Low urgency | ⚠️ Possible (e.g., "better code formatting") |
| Low frequency + High urgency | ⚠️ Possible (e.g., "legal contracts for startups") |
| Low frequency + Low urgency | ❌ Don't bother (e.g., "nice-to-have features") |

**The test:**
- Will people pay / switch / try imperfect solutions to fix this?
- If "yes" → urgent enough
- If "maybe later" → not urgent enough

#### C. Growth Trajectory
- **Is this problem getting bigger or smaller?**

**Look for:**
- ✅ Trends that amplify the problem (e.g., AI makes building easier → more people need distribution)
- ✅ New behaviors creating new needs (e.g., remote work → new collaboration tools)
- ❌ Problems that are declining (e.g., DVD rental → killed by streaming)

**Example: Super Distributor**

- **Market size:** Thousands of indie makers on X, Reddit, ProductHunt
- **Frequency:** Every time they launch (multiple times per year for serial builders)
- **Urgency:** High (they just spent months building, need users now)
- **Growth:** AI lowers barrier to building → more builders → bigger problem

**Validated: Problem is big enough.**

---

### Question 2: Is the Problem Hard Enough?

"Big" isn't enough. If the problem is easy to solve, competition will flood in and margins will compress.

**You want a problem that's hard enough to create a moat.**

#### A. Why Hasn't This Been Solved Already?

If it's obvious and easy, someone would've done it. Ask:

**Possible reasons:**
1. **Requires specific expertise** (technical, domain, or operational knowledge)
2. **Not profitable for big companies** (too small, too fragmented, low margins)
3. **Newly emerged** (technology or behavior change created the problem recently)
4. **Execution-intensive** (anyone can copy, but few can execute well)

**Example: Super Distributor**

- **Why unsolved?**
  - Big companies sell expensive marketing services (not free guides)
  - Influencers give generic advice (not step-by-step playbooks)
  - Requires builder-to-builder empathy (not just marketing knowledge)

- **Why hard?**
  - Needs real experience (can't fake having shipped products)
  - Needs curation and quality (easy to spam with bad advice)
  - Needs continuous updates (distribution tactics change)

**The moat:** Credibility + quality + community trust → hard to replicate quickly.

#### B. What Makes It Hard for You?

If it's hard for **you**, it's probably hard for competitors too. That's good.

But if it's **too hard**, you'll struggle to execute. That's bad.

**The sweet spot:**
- Hard enough that most people won't try
- But within your capability to solve

**Ask:**
- What skills / resources / insights does this require?
- Do you have most of them, or can you acquire them quickly?
- What's the 1-2 things that make this uniquely hard?

---

### Question 3: Are You the Best Person to Solve This?

This is the most important question — and the one most builders skip.

**It's not enough that the problem is big and hard. You need to be uniquely suited to solve it.**

#### A. Do You Have an Unfair Advantage?

An unfair advantage is something competitors can't easily copy:

**Types of unfair advantages:**
- **Domain expertise** (you deeply understand the problem because you lived it)
- **Unique access** (to users, data, distribution channels)
- **Credibility** (track record, reputation, audience)
- **Speed** (you can execute faster than others)
- **Insight** (you see the problem differently than everyone else)

**Example: Super Distributor (Grace's advantages)**

- **Domain expertise:** Shipped products, experienced distribution struggles firsthand
- **Builder credibility:** Building in public, proven track record
- **Unique insight:** Combining product-building skills with distribution knowledge (rare combo)
- **Execution speed:** Can ship playbooks quickly, iterate based on feedback

**If you don't have an unfair advantage, you're just another player in a crowded field.**

#### B. Can You Provide a Scarce Solution?

Scarcity creates value. If anyone can provide the same solution, it's a commodity.

**Your solution is scarce if:**
- Few people have your combination of skills
- Few people care enough to do the hard work
- Few people can maintain quality at scale

**Ask:**
- What makes your solution different from existing ones?
- Why can't competitors easily replicate it?
- What would it take for someone to build what you're building?

**Example: Super Distributor**

**Scarce because:**
- Most builders don't document their distribution learnings (too busy building)
- Most marketers don't understand indie builder constraints (different world)
- Quality + open-source + builder-to-builder is a rare combination

**Scarcity = value.**

## How to Find Your Structural Imbalance

Okay, you know what to look for. But **where** do you find these imbalances?

### Method 1: Follow Your Own Pain

The best problems to solve are ones **you've experienced personally**.

**Why:**
- You deeply understand the nuances (not surface-level)
- You have intuition for what good solutions look like
- You're intrinsically motivated (you wanted this to exist anyway)

**Ask yourself:**
- What problems have I encountered repeatedly in the past year?
- What solutions did I try that didn't work?
- What did I end up building for myself because nothing existed?

**Example:**
- Grace built products, struggled with distribution, learned through trial and error
- Realized others have the same problem
- Super Distributor = solving her own pain at scale

### Method 2: Observe Emerging Trends

Structural imbalances often appear when **something fundamental changes**:

**Types of change that create imbalances:**
- **Technology shifts** (e.g., AI making building easier → distribution becomes the bottleneck)
- **Behavior changes** (e.g., remote work → new tools needed)
- **Regulation** (e.g., GDPR → new compliance tools)
- **Economic shifts** (e.g., gig economy → freelancer tools)

**Use Market Intelligence (Playbook 01) to spot trends:**
- What are people talking about more this year than last year?
- What new complaints are emerging?
- What old solutions are breaking down?

### Method 3: Look for "Good Enough" Gaps

Often, the imbalance isn't "no solution exists." It's "solutions exist, but they're not good enough for a specific group."

**Signals of "good enough" gaps:**
- "I wish there was a version of X for Y" (e.g., "I wish there was Notion for [specific use case]")
- "X is too expensive / complex / bloated for what I need"
- "X works for big companies, but not for solo founders"

**This is where indie makers thrive** — serving neglected segments that big companies ignore.

**Example:**
- Marketing advice exists (for companies)
- Growth tools exist (expensive, complex)
- **Gap:** Simple, free, actionable distribution for indie makers

## Common Mistakes (And How to Avoid Them)

### Mistake 1: Falling in Love with Small Problems

**Problem:** The issue is real, but the market is too small to sustain a business (or even an open-source project).

**Example:**
- "A tool for left-handed drummers who code in Rust"
- The problem might be real, but there are 12 people in the world who fit this.

**Fix:** Validate market size before you commit. If you can't find 1,000+ people who care, reconsider.

---

### Mistake 2: Choosing Problems That Are Too Easy

**Problem:** Low barrier = high competition = commoditization.

**Example:**
- "A to-do list app" (anyone can build this in a weekend)

**Fix:** Look for problems that require specific expertise, unique access, or sustained effort. Easy problems attract too many competitors.

---

### Mistake 3: Solving Problems You Don't Understand

**Problem:** You see an imbalance in a domain you don't know well. You think "I can figure it out."

**Reality:** Without domain expertise, you'll build the wrong thing, market to the wrong people, and lose to competitors who actually get it.

**Example:**
- A developer building tools for lawyers without ever working in law
- Possible, but hard mode

**Fix:** Stick to problems you deeply understand, or spend significant time immersing yourself in the domain first.

---

### Mistake 4: Ignoring Your Unfair Advantage

**Problem:** You pick a problem where you have no edge. You're competing on equal footing with everyone else.

**Example:**
- Building a generic SaaS in a crowded space, with no unique insight or access

**Fix:** Choose problems where your specific background, skills, or network give you an advantage.

## Your Problem Validation Checklist

Before you move to positioning, make sure you can answer YES to all of these:

**Big Enough:**
- [ ] At least 1,000+ people have this problem
- [ ] The problem is frequent or urgent (not occasional and low-priority)
- [ ] The problem is growing or staying constant (not declining)

**Hard Enough:**
- [ ] I can explain why this hasn't been solved well yet
- [ ] There's a moat (expertise, execution, access, or insight required)
- [ ] Competitors can't easily replicate my solution

**Right Person:**
- [ ] I have personal experience with this problem
- [ ] I have an unfair advantage (domain expertise, access, credibility, or speed)
- [ ] I can provide a scarce solution (few others can do what I'm doing)

**If you can't check all these boxes, go back and find a different problem.**

The best positioning, naming, and distribution won't save you if you picked the wrong problem to solve.
