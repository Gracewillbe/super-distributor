# Social Media: Building Distribution Through Real Conversations

> The biggest mistake indie makers make on social media isn't posting too little — it's treating it like a broadcast channel instead of a conversation.

## The Old Playbook No Longer Works

Five years ago, the social media playbook was simple: post consistently, use the right hashtags, and the algorithm would reward you with reach. Growth hackers built empires on engagement pods, follower exchanges, and viral tricks.

Today, that playbook is broken.

Platforms have become saturated. Every creator is posting, every brand is "authentic," and every algorithm update makes organic reach harder to predict. The average post gets seen by less than 5% of your followers. Cold outreach messages get ignored. Viral moments are lottery tickets, not strategies.

This isn't a small change. It's structural.

The barrier to getting attention on social media hasn't just risen — it's fundamentally changed. **Volume doesn't win anymore. Connection does.**

We are entering an age of **attention scarcity meets authenticity hunger**.

## Three New Realities for Indie Makers

When everyone is shouting into the void, three questions now determine whether your social media efforts translate into actual distribution:

### 1. Are you building relationships or collecting followers?

A thousand followers who don't know you exist are worth less than ten people who actually care about your work. The temptation is to optimize for metrics — followers, likes, impressions. But distribution happens through **trust**, not reach. The discipline of building real relationships becomes more valuable than the ability to game algorithms.

### 2. Are you speaking to everyone or someone?

Generic posts about productivity, motivation, or "lessons learned" blend into the noise. In a world flooded with content, only **specific, opinionated, and useful** posts break through. The gap between "this could apply to anyone" and "this was written for me" has never been wider. Specificity — the act of speaking directly to a real person with a real problem — is now the critical skill.

### 3. Are you showing up as yourself or as a brand?

Users are exhausted by corporate speak and polished personas. They can smell inauthenticity from a mile away. This creates an opportunity: whoever shows up as a **real person with real struggles and real wins** earns attention. Not because they're perfect, but because they're human.

## Social Media Is a Bridge, Not a Stage

The old mental model was simple:

> Post content → Get followers → Convert to customers.

That model is dead.

Today, the equation looks like this:

> **Social media is where you earn permission to continue the conversation elsewhere.**

This is uncomfortable for builders. We like to believe that good content speaks for itself. Sometimes it does — but increasingly, it doesn't. A mediocre post that starts a real conversation will outperform a perfect post that gets ignored. Not because quality doesn't matter, but because **attention without connection delivers zero value**.

The evidence is everywhere:
- Indie makers who break out almost always have built a community before they launched.
- The most successful product launches start with "I've been sharing my journey for months."
- The common thread in every "overnight success" story is a long runway of relationship-building.

Social media is not advertising. It's not content marketing. It's not growth hacking.

**Social media is how you earn the right to be in someone's inbox, calendar, or consideration set.**

## The Super Distributor Approach to Social Media

This playbook exists because social media shouldn't feel like shouting into the void.

The strategies, templates, and mental models for effective social presence are learnable. They can be broken down into steps. They can be systematized. They can be shared openly.

Here's what works in 2025:

### 1. Pick One Platform and Go Deep

You don't need to be everywhere. You need to be **somewhere consistently**. Choose the platform where your people actually are:
- **Twitter/X**: For builders, developers, and product people
- **LinkedIn**: For B2B, enterprise, and professional services
- **Reddit**: For niche communities and early feedback
- **Instagram/TikTok**: For visual products and consumer audiences

Master one platform before spreading thin across five.

### 2. Document, Don't Create

Stop trying to be a "content creator." Just **share what you're already doing**:
- Building in public: "Today I shipped X, here's what I learned"
- Problem-solving: "I was stuck on X, here's how I solved it"
- Questions: "Has anyone dealt with X? I'm trying to figure out Y"

The best content comes from your actual work, not from trying to manufacture insights.

### 3. Start Conversations, Not Broadcasts

Every post should invite a response:
- **Ask questions**: "What's your experience with X?"
- **Share incomplete thoughts**: "I'm thinking about X, but I'm not sure..."
- **Be specific**: "For anyone building X, here's a problem I just hit..."

The goal isn't to sound smart. It's to **start a conversation that continues in DMs, comments, or email**.

### 4. The 10-Person Rule

Before you post anything, ask: "Would I send this directly to 10 specific people I know?"

If the answer is no, the post isn't specific enough. If yes, **actually send it to those 10 people** and ask for their feedback before posting publicly.

Distribution starts small. Every viral post began with the first person who shared it.

### 5. Respond More Than You Post

The fastest way to build presence: **comment thoughtfully on other people's posts**. Not generic "great post!" replies, but real responses that add value.

Spend 80% of your social media time engaging with others, 20% posting your own content. Relationships are built in replies, not reach.
