# Competitor Analyzer

Map your competitive landscape and find the gap you can own.

Based on **Chapter 03 - Positioning** from Super Distributor playbooks.

---

## What This Skill Does

Helps you systematically analyze competitors to find your unique positioning:
1. **Map the competitive landscape** - Who are the alternatives?
2. **Identify what positions are taken** - What do competitors own in users' minds?
3. **Find the gap** - Where's the white space?
4. **Get positioning recommendations** - How to differentiate

---

## Step 1: Define Your Product

Let's start with your product so we can identify relevant competitors.

**Question 1: What problem does your product solve?**
- Be specific. What pain point do users already recognize?
- Example: "Indie makers build products but don't know how to get users"

**Question 2: Who is your target user?**
- Be narrow, not broad
- Example: "Indie makers and solo founders" (not "everyone who needs marketing")

**Question 3: What is your product?**

## Step 4: Find the Gap

Based on the competitive matrix, let's identify positioning opportunities.

### Gap Analysis

I'll look for three types of gaps:

**1. White Space (Positions No One Owns)**
- Needs that exist but no product addresses
- Underserved user segments
- Overlooked use cases

**2. Weak Positions (Claimed But Not Delivered)**
- Competitors say they do X, but users complain about it
- Promises not kept
- Opportunities to do it better

**3. Abandoned Positions (Used to Exist, Now Neglected)**
- Solutions that existed but went away
- Positions competitors moved away from
- Nostalgic users looking for "the old way"

## Step 6: Positioning Validation Checklist

Before you lock in your positioning, test it:

### The 5-Second Test
- [ ] Show your positioning line to someone who doesn't know your product
- [ ] Wait 5 seconds, take it away
- [ ] Ask them to repeat what you do
- [ ] **Pass:** They can repeat it. **Fail:** It's too complex.

### The "Why You?" Test
- [ ] Ask a potential user: "Why would you use this instead of [competitor]?"
- [ ] Their answer should match your differentiator
- [ ] **Pass:** Answer matches. **Fail:** Positioning isn't clear.

### The Memory Test
- [ ] A week later, ask someone what your product does
- [ ] **Pass:** They remember correctly. **Fail:** They forgot or confused it.

### The Differentiation Test
- [ ] Could a competitor say the exact same thing?
- [ ] **Pass:** No, this is uniquely yours. **Fail:** Too generic, needs sharpening.

## Common Positioning Mistakes to Avoid

❌ **Being for everyone**
- "Perfect for startups, enterprises, freelancers, and students"
- **Fix:** Pick one. Own that niche first.

❌ **Feature soup**
- Listing what you have, not what position you own
- **Fix:** Lead with the outcome, not the method.

❌ **Claiming a crowded position**
- "The best project management tool"
- **Fix:** Find a specific angle no one owns.

❌ **Inventing a new category**
- "Revolutionary new paradigm for..."
- **Fix:** Position within an existing category users understand.

❌ **Copying competitor positioning**
- "Like Notion, but..." (you're now #2 in the "Notion" category)
- **Fix:** Own a different position entirely.

## Need Help?

- **Struggling to find competitors?** Google "[your problem] solutions", check Product Hunt, ask in Reddit/X communities
- **All positions seem taken?** Look for underserved segments or new angles on old problems
- **Positioning feels too narrow?** That's good. Narrow = sharp. You can expand later.

**Remember: Positioning is commitment. Pick one position and own it. Don't try to be everything.**
